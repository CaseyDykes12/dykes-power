Running beef cattle in Lincoln or Lawrence County means the mowing never really stops from April through October. Bahia grass in a wet year puts on six inches in a week. Bermuda in a managed pasture needs the edges trimmed regularly. And the pond banks, the stretch along fence rows, the area by the loading chute — that work adds up fast.

A residential zero-turn won't handle ranch terrain. The banks are too steep, the ground is too rough, and the hours are too long. Commercial machines built to run all day on uneven ground are what actually holds up.

## What Ranch Terrain Looks Like in South Mississippi

Cattle country around Brookhaven and Monticello rolls. Creek drainages, terraced pastures, cut-off ends of fields that collect runoff. Pond banks drop off steep enough that the wrong mower will slide. Fence lines have compaction ruts from years of tractor traffic. Gate areas have gravel and rock mixing into the edge of the grass.

Bahia also grows differently than Bermuda. It's tougher, coarser, harder on blades. If you're mowing Bahia-heavy pasture edges at any real volume, blade wear is a real cost you factor in.

Most ranch operators running a zero turn are on the machine two to four hours at a stretch. The terrain is not even. You're working slopes, rough headlands, and areas that were never graded.

## Deck Size for Ranch Work

If your operation is mostly open ground — long runs along pasture fence lines, pond banks, access roads — the 60" deck is what you want. More ground covered per pass, and fence line work doesn't have the kind of tight spots that demand a smaller cut.

If you've got corral approaches, narrow gates, or a lot of tight work around equipment sheds, a 52" deck gives you more room without giving up too much on productivity. Some ranches keep a 60" for the open work and a smaller machine or finish mower for the tight spots.

## Maintenance Reality

Bahia is hard on blades. Plan on sharpening or replacing more often than you would on Bermuda — sometimes twice as often in a heavy Bahia year. The Ferris iCD™ cutting system uses a fabricated deck with overlapping blade arcs; spindles are greaseable and built for high-hour commercial use.

Regular fluid checks on the transaxles and air filter swaps on any air-cooled engine running in July heat are not optional. A commercial mower that runs through a Mississippi summer without attention won't make it to fall.
